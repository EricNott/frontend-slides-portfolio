About Me

I chose engineering because I want to create something new. To achieve this, I have incorporated physics into my academic rigor, as I believe that creating something novel requires bridging the science and abstract thought of physics with the application of engineering. While this goal is ambitious, I have decided to break it down into more achievable pieces.

Professionally, I believe it's essential to gain a deeper understanding of various industries and how each sector operates. I value learning every aspect of the job site and applying ethics to every project.

When analyzing failure modes, I quantify them. If a laser safety sensor fails to trigger, I can calculate the relationship between rotational speed and pulse rate to determine the minimum detectable thickness. I prefer to understand what is happening inside the "black box" before trusting it, and this approach has made me interdisciplinary by nature.

Through my time at Northeastern, I have intentionally worked across domains. I have self-taught C/C++ on the job to reverse-engineer MFC code for GUI design, written MATLAB scripts for FFT-based modal analysis, and worked with various robotics tools. What motivates me is not the tool itself, but the transferable method behind it. I look for principles that can be abstracted and reused across systems.

I am now seeking a position focused on research and experimental design to further apply critical thinking. Alternatively, I am interested in a start-up environment to gain insight into how science can be applied in a fast-paced collaborative team dynamic.

MFC Data Acquisition
GUI

Airport security scanners are required to maintain consistent throughput, making reliability critical. Ball bearings within rotating drum assemblies are high-wear components and were being evaluated on a life-testing platform to better understand failure modes.

Objectives
  • Integrate ball bearing and "truck" lifetime monitoring into existing machine health metrics (temperature, RPM, throughput, jam detection) by determining whether accelerometer data could serve as an early indicator of bearing degradation.
Process
  • Being more of a R&D project, the aim was to first determine correlation between vibration (peak to peak g-values) and bearing failure behavior
  • Reverse-engineered an existing two-channel histogram-based GUI
  • Extended architecture to support four simultaneous channels for monitoring additional trucks
  • Parsed serial data streams from embedded hardware
  • Histograms were saved hourly, recording data once drum has reached steady state; most populated bin (dominant vibrational bin) was logged per hour for each truck to be trended over time

Pilot Valve Accumulator
Test Fixture

New simulator needed to be built, but legacy documentation was incomplete. The existing test site could not achieve the operating pressure required to actuate the pilot valve, nor accommodate the diameter of the full-scale valve assembly.

Objectives
  • Design a compact pressure accumulation simulator to reliably trigger the smaller pilot valve under representative operating conditions. Functionally, the system is analogous to a capacitor in an electrical circuit — storing pressure energy and releasing it to produce a controlled actuation event.
Design Constraints
  • Compliance with material limits and standard pressure ratings
